Review article

A review of physics-based low-temperature proton-exchange membrane fuel
cell models for system-level water and thermal management studies
Pedro Henrique Affonso Nóbrega
Mines Paris, PSL University, Centre for processes, renewable energy and energy systems (PERSEE), 06904 Sophia Antipolis, France



HIGHLIGHTS

• Review of low computational cost physics-based PEMFC numerical models.
• Focus on transport phenomena impacting water and thermal management performance.
• Analysis of 54 models with different levels of detail and modelling hypotheses.
• Comparison of model validation practices and computational time.


ARTICLE               INFO                               ABSTRACT

Keywords:                                                Water management and thermal management are essential for the design and operation of low-temperature
Proton-exchange membrane                                 proton-exchange membrane fuel cells and demand a deep understanding of the fuel cell system behaviour.
Fuel cell                                                System-level studies require fast and simple numerical models, a challenge given the complexity of the
Physics-based model
                                                         transport phenomena involved. Numerous models exist in the literature, with different levels of complexity and
Water management
                                                         different hypotheses aiming at simplifying the representation of transport phenomena. This review summarizes
Thermal management
                                                         different hypotheses and modelling approaches adopted in low computational cost physics-based proton-
                                                         exchange membrane fuel cell models, with a focus on aspects related to water and thermal management.
                                                         It also describes modelling approaches for auxiliary system components, validation practices and compare
                                                         the computational efficiency of different models. We put in evidence the high diversity of hypotheses and
                                                         modelling approaches adopted in the literature, which reflect the trade-off between computational cost, model
                                                         complexity and accuracy. We conclude that rather than developing new models from scratch, a strong effort
                                                         on the validation of the existing models is needed, with the development of a standard validation procedure.

    Fuel cell continuum models can be classified according to the spatial
dimensions considered. The so-called 0D models consider the anode
and the cathode as a single control volume each behaving like a
continuously-stirred tank reactor, without any spatial consideration. A
typical 0D model is schematized in Fig. 1 extracted from Ritzberger
et al. [27]. 0D models can use empirical or semi-empirical correlations
to overcome the lack of spatial detail, but they are not able to describe
spatial phenomena occurring inside the anode or the cathode. Further,
0D models have limited prediction capability as they must often be
calibrated for a specific material, under specific operating conditions.
    1D models adopt a spatial discretization with more than one control
volume along either the flow channel (along-the-channel) direction or
the through-plane direction of the cell. These discretizations allow the
consideration of transport phenomena, but increase the computational
cost. Through-plane 1D models focus on the transport phenomena
occurring across the different layers of the MEA and allow to estab-
lish through-plane profiles of quantities such as gas concentrations,
temperature or liquid water saturation. They contribute to a better
understanding of these phenomena, as well as the impact of operating
conditions and material properties. Along-the-channel 1D models focus
on the effects of the changes in flow conditions in the gas flow channels
as reactants are consumed and product water evacuated, and specially                     Fig. 1. Schematics of the 0D model used by Ritzberger et al. [27].
